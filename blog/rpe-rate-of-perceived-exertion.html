An Athlete's Introduction to Rating of Perceived Exertion (RPE)

By Jon Parkin · October 16, 2019 · 5 min read

This article drives 5,070 sessions per quarter — 15% of all site traffic

If you've heard the term RPE thrown around at training and weren't sure what it meant — this is for you. Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE) is a useful way to quantify your training load and combine with GPS loading.

What is RPE?

RPE is an excellent tool for gauging both your exercise intensity and how your fitness level progresses over time. It's a single number (out of 10) to rate any given training session or competitive match.

Your RPE score can then be multiplied by the duration (in minutes) of any given performance, which will provide you with an arbitrary score of load. This is one of the simplest yet most powerful tools in sports science.

The RPE Scale

The classic model uses a 1-10 scale, though methods such as the modified Borg Scale (6-20) have also been employed in team sport environments. The key is consistency — pick a scale and stick with it.

Best practice: Score immediately post-session. Consider the entire session, not just the final drill. In team environments, be careful of athletes hearing how others rate the session — this can bias scores. Providing visual scoring scales helps ensure accuracy.

RPE & Performance

RPE becomes an easily administered tool that can guide your training practices. Combined with external measures like GPS devices, it facilitates balanced training between high physical demands and skill acquisition.

For example, a coach might plan a high-intensity running session (RPE target: 8-9) on Tuesday, followed by a tactical/skills session (RPE target: 4-5) on Wednesday. GPS data validates whether the actual intensity matched the plan.

Planned vs. Actual RPE

There are two approaches to RPE assessment:

1. Simple method: Assign weekly RPE totals across all sessions and competition. Track the trend over time — is total load increasing, stable, or decreasing?

2. Comparative method: Establish a planned RPE before sessions, then compare against actual RPE afterwards. If planned was 7 but actual was 9, the session was harder than intended — investigate why and adjust.

RPE & Return to Performance

For injured athletes resuming training, RPE is critical. The recommendation is to undertake weekly loads that are fairly below what you think you're capable of. Start conservative:

  • Week 1-2: RPE 4-5 sessions, reduced duration (30-40 min)
  • Week 3-4: RPE 5-6, normal duration, limited high-speed running
  • Week 5+: Gradual return to full training RPE with GPS monitoring

GPS data is essential here — it gives objective validation that the athlete's perceived effort matches their actual output. An athlete might feel fine (RPE 5) but GPS shows their high-speed running metres are 40% below their pre-injury baseline. That gap is critical information.

Key Takeaway

RPE is free, takes 10 seconds, and when combined with GPS tracking data, gives coaches and athletes a complete picture of training load. The combination of subjective (how it felt) and objective (what actually happened) data is the gold standard in modern sports science.

Start simple: Record RPE for every session. After 4 weeks, you'll have enough data to spot patterns, prevent overtraining, and make smarter decisions about when to push and when to rest.
