How to Track Player Load in Pre-Season

March 15, 2026 · 6 min read

Pre-season is where championships are won or lost. GPS tracking gives coaches the data they need to push players hard enough to improve — without crossing the line into injury. Here's how to use GPS data to manage training loads effectively.

Why Load Monitoring Matters

The single biggest predictor of soft tissue injury in team sports is rapid spikes in training load. Research consistently shows that when a player's acute workload (last 7 days) exceeds 1.5× their chronic workload (last 28 days), injury risk increases dramatically.

GPS tracking makes this measurable. Instead of guessing whether training is "too hard" or "not enough," you get objective data: total distance, high-speed running metres, sprint counts, and player load scores — for every player, every session.

The Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio

The acute:chronic workload ratio (ACWR) compares your recent training load to your historical average. Here's how to calculate it:

  • Acute load = total load from the last 7 days
  • Chronic load = average weekly load over the last 28 days
  • ACWR = Acute / Chronic
  • Sweet spot: 0.8 – 1.3 — this is where fitness improves without excessive injury risk
  • Danger zone: above 1.5 — injury risk increases significantly

With GPS tracking, you can calculate this for every metric — total distance, high-speed running, sprint count — and for every player individually.

Pre-Season Loading Strategy

A well-structured pre-season progressively increases load over 6-8 weeks. Here's a general framework:

Weeks 1-2: Base building — moderate volume, low intensity. Target ACWR around 0.8.

Weeks 3-4: Progressive overload — increase total distance by 10-15% per week. ACWR approaching 1.0.

Weeks 5-6: Match-intensity sessions — introduce high-speed running and sprint volume. ACWR 1.0-1.2.

Weeks 7-8: Taper and match simulation — reduce volume, maintain intensity. ACWR settling back to 1.0.

Key Metrics to Track

Not all GPS metrics are created equal for pre-season monitoring. Focus on these:

  • Total distance — the base volume indicator
  • High-speed running (>16 km/h) — the primary injury risk metric for hamstrings
  • Sprint count (>20 km/h) — maximal effort exposure
  • Player load — overall mechanical stress including accelerations and decelerations
  • Metres per minute — intensity indicator, useful for comparing sessions of different durations

Getting Started

You don't need a sports science degree to use GPS data effectively. Modern platforms like GameTraka (included free with every SPT device) calculate ACWR automatically and flag when players are entering the danger zone.

Start with total distance and high-speed running. Get consistent data across 4+ weeks. Then layer in sprint counts and player load as your team gets comfortable with the data.

The most important step? Just start tracking. Imperfect data is infinitely better than no data.
